(![sa](langx){.क्लेश}; ![pi](langx){.किलेस} \'\'kilesa\'\';
![bo](langx){.ཉོན་མོངས།} \'\'nyon mongs\'\'), in
[Buddhism](Buddhism){.wikilink}, are mental states that cloud the mind
and manifest in unwholesome actions. \'\'Kleshas\'\' include states of
mind such as anxiety, fear, anger, jealousy, desire, etc. Contemporary
translators use a variety of English words to translate the term
\'\'kleshas\'\', such as: afflictions, defilements, destructive
emotions, disturbing emotions, negative emotions, mind poisons, and
neuroses.

In the contemporary Mahayana and [Theravada](Theravada){.wikilink}
Buddhist traditions, the three kleshas of ignorance, attachment, and
aversion are identified as the root or source of all other kleshas.
These are referred to as the \'\'[three
poisons](Three poisons (Buddhism)){.wikilink}\'\' in the Mahayana
tradition, or as the three \'\'unwholesome roots\'\' in the Theravada
tradition.

While the [early Buddhist texts](early Buddhist texts){.wikilink} of the
[Pali Canon](Pali Canon){.wikilink} do not specifically enumerate the
three root kleshas, the \'\'three poisons\'\' (and the kleshas
generally) came to be seen as the very roots of [samsaric
existence](Saṃsāra (Buddhism)){.wikilink}.

## Pali literature {#Pali literature}

![](PaliCanon) In the [Pali Canon](Pali Canon){.wikilink}\'s discourses
(\'\'[sutta](Sūtra){.wikilink}\'\'), \'\'kilesa\'\' is often associated
with the various passions that defile bodily and mental states. In the
Pali Canon\'s [Abhidhamma](Abhidhamma Pitaka){.wikilink} and
post-canonical [Pali literature](Pali literature){.wikilink}, ten
defilements are identified, the first three of which -- greed, hatred,
delusion -- are considered to be the \"roots\" of suffering.

### Sutta Piṭaka: mental hindrances {#Sutta Piṭaka: mental hindrances}

In the Pali Canon\'s [Sutta Piṭaka](Sutta Piṭaka){.wikilink},
\'\'kilesa\'\' and its correlate \'\'upakkilesa\'\'\<ref\>Beyond the
etymological relationship between and semantic closeness of
\'\'kilesa\'\' and \'\'upakkilesa\'\' (e.g., see Rhys Davids & Stede,
1921--25, p. 139, entry for \'\'upakkilesa\'\' at
\[<https://archive.today/20120723015604/http://dsal.uchicago.edu/cgi-bin/philologic/getobject.pl?c.0:1:3657.pali>\]),
the below-referenced [Samyutta Nikaya](Samyutta Nikaya){.wikilink}
collection entitled \"\'\'\<u\>Kilesa\</u\>-sa![ṃ](IAST)yutta\'\'\" (SN
27) does not use \'\'kilesa\'\' in its actual suttas but, in fact,
\'\'\<u\>upakkilesa\</u\>\'\'. Bodhi (2000), pp. 1012--14, 1100
\'\'n\'\'. 273, specifically makes note of the lexical differences
between these two Pali words and chooses to translate \'\'kilesa\'\' as
\"defilement\" and \'\'upakkilesa\'\' as \"corruption.\" Similar, in
Bodhi (2000), p. 1642, SN 47.12, \'\'upakkilesa\'\' is translated as
\"corruption\" whereas, as indicated below, in Bodhi (2005), p. 416,
this same Pali word in the same sutta is translated as \"defilement.\"
Consistent with Bodhi (2005), as seen below, Thanissaro (1994) also
translates \'\'upakkilesa\'\' as \"defilement.\"

The related correlate \'\'sankilesa\'\' (or \'\'sa![ṅ](IAST)kilesa\'\')
is also translated as \"defilement\" by Bodhi (e.g., 2000, pp. 903--4;
2005, pp. 55--6),
\[<http://www.accesstoinsight.org/tipitaka/mn/mn.026.than.html>
Thanissaro (2004)\] and Rhys Davids & Stede (1921-5,
\[<https://archive.today/20120708064129/http://dsal.uchicago.edu/cgi-bin/philologic/getobject.pl?c.3:1:2756.pali>
entry for \"Sankilesa\"\]). In [SN](Samyutta Nikaya){.wikilink} 22.60
(Bodhi, 2000, pp. 903--4), \'\'sankilesa\'\' is contextualized by: \"By
being enamoured with \[form\], \[beings\] are captivated by it, and by
being captivated by it they are defiled.\" In this sutta,
\'\'sankilesa\'\' is juxtaposed with purification (\'\'visuddhi\'\')
which is contextualized by: \"Experiencing revulsion \[in the
impermanence of form\'s pleasure\], \[beings\] become dispassionate, and
through dispassion they are purified.\"\</ref\> are affective obstacles
to the pursuit of direct knowledge
(\'\'[abhijñā](abhijñā){.wikilink}\'\') and wisdom
(\'\'[pañña](pañña){.wikilink}\'\').

For instance, the [Samyutta Nikaya](Samyutta Nikaya){.wikilink} includes
a collection of ten discourses ([SN](Samyutta Nikaya){.wikilink} 27,
\'\'Kilesa-sa![ṃ](IAST)yutta\'\') that state that any association of
\"desire-passion\" (\'\'chanda-rāgo\'\') with the body or mind\<ref\>In
particular, this \'\'sa![ṃ](IAST)yutta\'\' contextualizes \'\'kilesa\'\'
vis-à-vis the six internal and external \"sense bases\"
(\'\'[ayatana](ayatana){.wikilink}\'\') and their mental concomitants
(the six classes of [consciousness](vijnana){.wikilink},
[contact](phassa){.wikilink}, [feeling](vedana){.wikilink} and
[craving](Taṇhā){.wikilink}, see the section on the [\"six
sextets\"](Ayatana#Sense-base contexts){.wikilink}), the six primary
\"elements\" (\'\'dhātu\'\', cf.
\'\'[mahābhūta](mahābhūta){.wikilink}\'\'), and the five \"aggregates\"
(\'\'[khandha](khandha){.wikilink}\'\').\</ref\> is a \"defilement of
mind\" (\'\'cittasse\'so upakkileso\'\'): :\"Monks, any desire-passion
with regard to the eye is a defilement of the mind. Any desire-passion
with regard to the ear\... the nose\... the tongue\... the body\... the
intellect is a defilement of the mind. When, with regard to [these six
bases](ayatana){.wikilink}, the defilements of awareness are abandoned,
then the mind is inclined to [renunciation](nekkhamma){.wikilink}. The
mind fostered by renunciation feels malleable for the [direct
knowing](abhijna){.wikilink} of those [qualities](dhamma){.wikilink}
worth realizing.\"\<ref\>[SN](Samyutta Nikaya){.wikilink} 27.1
\[<http://www.accesstoinsight.org/tipitaka/sn/sn27/sn27.001-010.than.html>
(trans. Thanissaro, 1994).\] Note that the phrase that Thanissaro
translates as \"defilement of \<u\>awareness\</u\>\" here is
\'\'\<u\>ceta\</u\>so upakkileso\'\'; Bodhi (2000), p. 1012, simply

### Abhidhamma: ten defilements and unwholesome roots {#Abhidhamma: ten defilements and unwholesome roots}

While the Sutta Pitaka does not offer a list of \'\'kilesa\'\', the
[Abhidhamma Pitaka](Abhidhamma Pitaka){.wikilink}\'s
[Dhammasangani](Dhammasangani){.wikilink} (Dhs. 1229\'\'ff\'\'.) and
[Vibhanga](Vibhanga){.wikilink} (Vbh. XII) as well as in the
post-canonical [Visuddhimagga](Visuddhimagga){.wikilink} (Vsm. XXII 49,
65) enumerate ten defilements (\'\'dasa kilesa-vatthūni\'\') as follows:

1.  greed (\'\'[lobha](Raga (Buddhism)){.wikilink}\'\')
2.  hatred (\'\'[dosa](Dvesha (Buddhism)){.wikilink}\'\')
3.  delusion (\'\'[moha](Moha (Buddhism)){.wikilink}\'\')
4.  conceit (\'\'[māna](māna){.wikilink}\'\')
5.  wrong views (\'\'[micchāditthi](View (Buddhism)){.wikilink}\'\')
6.  doubt (\'\'[vicikicchā](vicikicchā){.wikilink}\'\')
7.  torpor (\'\'[thīna![ṃ](IAST)](thīna){.wikilink}\'\')
8.  restlessness (\'\'[uddhacca![ṃ](IAST)](uddhacca){.wikilink}\'\')
9.  shamelessness (\'\'[ahirika![ṃ](IAST)](Āhrīkya){.wikilink}\'\')
10. recklessness
    (\'\'[anottappa![ṃ](IAST)](anottappa){.wikilink}\'\')\<ref\>Rhys
    Davids & Stede (1921--5), p. 217; and, Nyanatiloka (1988), entry for
    \"kilesa,\" retrieved 2008-02-09 from \"BuddhaSasana\" at
    <http://www.buddhanet.net/budsas/ebud/bud-dict/dic3_k.htm>
    ![url=<https://web.archive.org/web/20120328192559/http://www.buddhanet.net/budsas/ebud/bud-dict/dic3_k.htm>
    ](Webarchive).\</ref\>

The Vibhanga also includes an eightfold list (\'\'a![ṭṭ](IAST)ha
kilesa-vatthūni\'\') composed of the first eight of the above
ten.\<ref\>Rhys Davids & Stede (1921--25), p. 217.\</ref\>

Throughout Pali literature, the first three \'\'kilesa\'\' in the above
tenfold Abhidhamma list (\'\'lobha dosa moha\'\') are known as the
\"unwholesome roots\" (\'\'akusala-mūla\'\' or the root of
[akusala](Buddhist views on sin){.wikilink}); and, their opposites
(\'\'alobha adosa amoha\'\') are the three \"wholesome roots\"
(\'\'kusala-mūla\'\' or the root of
[kusala](Merit (Buddhism)#Puñña, kusala and Nirvana){.wikilink}).\<ref\>In
addition to frequent reference in the Abhidhamma and post-canonical Pali
literature, references to the unwholesome roots (\'\'akusala-mūla\'\')
are sprinkled throughout the [Sutta Pitaka](Sutta Pitaka){.wikilink}.
For instance, in the [Digha Nikaya](Digha Nikaya){.wikilink}, it can be
found in DN 33 (D iii.215) and DN 34 (D iii.275); in the
[Majjhima Nikaya](Majjhima Nikaya){.wikilink}, it is the first of
several topics discussed by Ven. [Sariputta](Sariputta){.wikilink} in
the well-known \'\'[Sammādi![ṭṭ](IAST)hi
Sutta](Sammaditthi Sutta){.wikilink}\'\' (\"Right View Discourse,\" MN
9); and, in the [Itivuttaka](Itivuttaka){.wikilink}, a brief discourse
on three unwholesome roots starts off the \"Section of the Threes\"
(Iti. 50). However, in \'\'none\'\' of these Sutta Pitaka texts are the
three unwholesome roots referred to as \'\'kilesa\'\'. Such an
association appears to begin in the Abhidhamma texts.\</ref\> The
presence of such a wholesome or unwholesome root during a mental, verbal
or bodily action conditions future states of consciousness and
associated mental factors (see
[Karma](Karma (Buddhism)){.wikilink}).\<ref\>Nyanatiloka (1988), entry
for \"mūla,\" retrieved 2008-02-09 from \"BuddhaSasana\" at
<http://www.buddhanet.net/budsas/ebud/bud-dict/dic3_m.htm>.\</ref\>

298) divides the twelve factors into three \"rounds\"
(\'\'va![ṭṭ](IAST)a\'\'):

- the \"round of defilements\" (\'\'kilesa-va![ṭṭ](IAST)a\'\')
- the \"round of [kamma](Karma in Buddhism){.wikilink}\"
  (\'\'kamma-va![ṭṭ](IAST)a\'\')

  (\'\'[vipāka](vipaka){.wikilink}-va![ṭṭ](IAST)a\'\').\<ref
  name=\"Vsm_xvii_298\" /\>\<ref\>Cf. the
  [paracanonical](Paracanonical texts (Theravada Buddhism)){.wikilink}
  [Nettipakara![ṇ](IAST)a](Nettipakarana){.wikilink}\'s \"round of
  suffering, round of action, round of defilements\"
  (\'\'dukkhava![ṭṭ](IAST)o kammava![ṭṭ](IAST)o
  kilesava![ṭṭ](IAST)o\'\') (Nett.
  i.95).![url=<http://www.bodhgayanews.net/tipitaka.php?title=&record=15231>
  ](cite web )\</ref\>

In this framework (see Figure to the right, starting from the bottom of
the Figure), \'\'kilesa\'\'
([\"ignorance\"](Avidya (Buddhism)){.wikilink}) conditions \'\'kamma\'\'
([\"formations\"](sankhara){.wikilink}) which conditions
[results](vipaka){.wikilink} ([\"consciousness\"](vinnana){.wikilink}
through [\"feelings\"](vedana){.wikilink}) which in turn condition
\'\'kilesa\'\' ([\"craving\"](Taṇhā){.wikilink} and
[\"clinging\"](upadana){.wikilink}) which condition \'\'kamma\'\'
([\"becoming\"](bhava){.wikilink}) and so on.\<ref
name=\"Vsm_xvii_297\"\>Strictly speaking, in this framework the
[Visuddhimagga](Visuddhimagga){.wikilink} (Vsm. XVII, 298) does not
\'\'explicitly\'\' identify \"birth\"
(\'\'[jāti](Jāti (Buddhism)){.wikilink}\'\') and \"aging-death\"

(\'\'[vipāka](vipāka){.wikilink}\'\'). Nonetheless, in the preceding

in original). Thus, \"birth\" and \"ageing and death\" become correlates
or expressions of the five-fold \"results\" sequence.\</ref\>
[Buddhaghosa](Buddhaghosa){.wikilink} (Vsm. XVII, 298) concludes: :So
this [Wheel of Becoming](Wheel of Becoming){.wikilink}, having a triple

As can be seen, in this framework, the round of defilements consists of:

- ignorance (\'\'[avijjā](avijjā){.wikilink}\'\')
- craving (\'\'[![taṇhā](IAST)](taṇhā){.wikilink}\'\')
- clinging (\'\'[upādāna](upadana){.wikilink}\'\').\<ref
  name=\"Vsm_xvii_298\" /\>

## Sanskrit Sravaka and Mahayana literature {#Sanskrit Sravaka and Mahayana literature}

### Three poisons {#Three poisons}

![Three poisons (Buddhism)](Main)

The three kleshas of ignorance, attachment and aversion are referred to
as the \'\'\'\'\'three poisons\'\'\'\'\' (Skt. \'\'triviṣa\'\') in the
Mahayana tradition and as the \'\'\'\'\'three unwholesome

are considered to be the root of all the other kleshas.

### Six root kleshas of the Abhidharma {#Six root kleshas of the Abhidharma}

The \'\'[Abhidharma-kośa](Abhidharma-kośa){.wikilink}\'\' identifies six
root kleshas (\'\'mūlakleśa\'\'):

- Attachment ([\'\'raga\'\'](Raga (Buddhism)){.wikilink})
- Anger (\'\'[pratigha](pratigha){.wikilink}\'\')
- Ignorance (\'\'[avidya](Avidya (Buddhism)){.wikilink}\'\')
- Pride/Conceit (\'\'[māna](māna){.wikilink}\'\')
- Doubt (\'\'[vicikitsa](vicikitsa){.wikilink}\'\')
- Wrong view/False view/Opinionatedness
  ([\'\'dṛiṣṭi\'\'](View (Buddhism)){.wikilink})\<ref name=g1\>Guenther
  (1975), Kindle Location 321.\</ref\>

In the context of the [Yogācāra](Yogacara){.wikilink} school of
Buddhism, Muller (2004: p.&nbsp;207) states that the Six Klesha arise
due to the \"\...reification of an \'imagined self\' (Sanskrit:
\'\'![satkāya-dṛṣṭi](IAST)\'\')\".\<ref\>\[<http://www.acmuller.net/articles/reinterpretations_of_the_hindrances.html>
Muller (2004).\]\</ref\>

### Mahaparinirvana Sutra {#Mahaparinirvana Sutra}

The
[Mahayana Mahaparinirvana Sutra](Mahayana Mahaparinirvana Sutra){.wikilink}
lists approximately 50 kleshas, including those of attachment, aversion,
stupidity, jealousy, pride, heedlessness, haughtiness, ill-will,
quarrelsomeness, wrong livelihood, deceit, consorting with immoral
friends, attachment to pleasure, to sleep, to eating, and to yawning;
delighting in excessive talking and uttering lies, as well as thoughts
of harm.![date=July 2023](citation needed)

### Two obscurations {#Two obscurations}

Mahayana literature often features an enumeration of \"two
obscurations\" (Wylie: \'\'sgrib gnyis\'\'), the \"obscuration of
conflicting emotions\" (Sanskrit: \'\'kleśa-avaraṇa\'\', Wylie:
\'\'nyon-mongs-pa\'i sgrib-ma\'\') and the \"obscuration concerning the
knowable\" (Sanskrit: \'\'jñeya-avaraṇa\'\', Wylie: \'\'shes-bya\'i
sgrib-ma\'\').\<ref\>Dorje, Jikdrel Yeshe
([Dudjom Rinpoche](Dudjom Rinpoche){.wikilink}, author), translated and
edited: [Gyurme Dorje](Gyurme Dorje){.wikilink} and
[Matthew Kapstein](Matthew Kapstein){.wikilink} (1991). \'\'The Nyingma
School of Tibetan Buddhism: Its Fundamentals and History\'\'. Boston,
USA: Wisdom Publications. ![0-86171-199-8](ISBN),
p.&nbsp;107(Enumerations).\</ref\>

## Overcoming the kleshas {#Overcoming the kleshas}

All Buddhist schools teach that through Tranquility
([Samatha](Samatha){.wikilink}) meditation the kilesas are pacified,
though not eradicated, and through Insight
([Vipassana](Vipassana){.wikilink}) the true nature of the kilesas and
the mind itself is understood. When the empty nature of the Self and the
Mind is fully understood, there is no longer a root for the disturbing
emotions to be attached to, and the disturbing emotions lose their power
to distract the mind.![date=May 2021](citation needed)
